The Problem

"Notifications are too easy to ignore. But something sitting on your desk — you can't ignore that."

That's what one RPI student told us during research, and it became the seed for everything. People aren't failing at habits because they don't care — they quit because the tools punish them for slipping, and starting over feels worse than stopping.

40% of users we surveyed still rely on physical journals. That's not nostalgia — it's a signal that apps alone aren't meeting the emotional need.

The Solution

Before landing on Moti-Track, we explored flashcards and analog note systems — all too passive. The pivot came from two products: TRMNL, an e-ink desk display, and LoveBox, a connected device couples use to send messages across distance through a spinning heart.

Both shared the same insight: a physical object in your space creates presence that a push notification never will. That became the Focus Block — and everything followed from there.

Research

What RPI students told us

We interviewed and surveyed students on campus — people actively trying to build habits, or who had tried and given up. No finished product to test yet, so this was about understanding the emotional landscape first.

The clearest pattern: people didn't quit because they stopped caring. They quit because the moment they missed a day, the app made them feel like they'd already failed.

01

Punishing UX kills momentum faster than missing a day does

Students said they stopped using an app not because they missed a habit — but because of how the app responded. A wiped streak felt like being told to start from zero.

02

Notifications are too easy to ignore

One student put it directly: "I just swipe them away without thinking." A physical object works differently — ambient, persistent, no demand for your attention.

03

People want accountability, not competition

Knowing a friend is also working on their habits — without being ranked against them — was consistently more motivating than any streak number or reward system.

04

The 40% journal stat isn't random

People who stuck with journals called them "gentler" and "more personal." That word kept coming up. It wasn't about features — it was about how the tool made them feel about themselves.

Defining the Theme

Resilience as a design principle

We were asked to design around a social issue. We chose motivation loss and emotional burnout. Resilience — recovering from setbacks rather than being defined by them — became the lens for every decision we made.

How might we
Automate enough of the habit loop so users don't carry the mental load of tracking manually?
How might we
Show progress in a way that feels honest and encouraging, not strict or shameful?
How might we
Use community as solidarity — not comparison — so users feel supported rather than judged?
How might we
Make a physical object part of the habit loop so accountability exists even when the phone is away?
01

Gentle over strict

No punishing resets. Every missed day is a reflection point, not a failure state.

02

Physical presence matters

A device you can see creates ambient accountability that push notifications can't replicate.

03

Community, not competition

Shared streaks build solidarity. Others' progress should inspire, not intimidate.

The Solution

Moti-Track — two systems, one goal

A mobile app for interactive tracking and reflection, and a physical Focus Block for ambient, always-on accountability.

Dashboard
Your day at a glance

The home screen shows your day streak, today's habit completion, and weekly progress — all in one view. Two stats that matter most are front and center so users never have to go looking.

Habit Tracker
Tracking that doesn't feel like a test you can fail

Each habit is a simple check-off. Completed habits are visually struck through so progress feels satisfying. Missing a habit doesn't wipe anything — it opens a reflection prompt instead.

Daily Reflection
Reflection over reset

Instead of wiping your streak when you miss a day, Moti-Track shows you your streak and prompts you to reflect. The streak persists — it just gets a note attached. This reframe was the most important design decision in the whole project.

Morning Quote
Start the day with intention

Every morning, users are greeted with a motivational quote and a summary of their streak, habits completed, and weekly goal percentage. The "Start Your Day" button is the first interaction — gentle, not demanding.

Physical Device
The Focus Block — always-on accountability

Inspired by LoveBox — a connected object tied to people you care about — the Focus Block sits on your desk and shows your streak alongside your community's. No notifications. No demands. Just presence.

  • 5 screens — 4 sides + 1 top for settings
  • 3D-printed frame in PLA or PETG
  • Embedded speaker + rechargeable battery
  • Wireless charging pad on bottom

Key Design Decisions

The choices that shaped the product

01

Reflection over reset

This came directly from what students told us. The moment an app wipes your streak, the math changes — starting over feels worse than quitting. So instead of resetting, Moti-Track prompts a journal entry. The streak doesn't disappear — it just has a note attached to that day.

02

A physical device, not just another app

The hardest part of this project was figuring out what the cube actually was. LoveBox helped me see it: a connected physical object that shows something meaningful without requiring you to open anything. The Focus Block sits on your desk and shows your streak and your friends' streaks — always visible, never demanding.

03

Community without competition

We talked about leaderboards. We decided against them because the research was clear — people want to feel like others are in it with them, not like they're losing a race. Seeing a friend's 12-day streak on the cube is motivating. Being ranked 8th out of 10 is demoralizing.

04

Designing without a working prototype

We never built a real Focus Block — the technology and time weren't there. That constraint forced us to be precise about what the experience should feel like conceptually, and to make sure the app was strong enough to stand on its own.

Reflection

What this project taught me

Learnings

The hardest part wasn't the Figma screens — it was figuring out what the cube actually should be. Once I found LoveBox and TRMNL, something clicked: the physical object needed to work like ambient presence, not an alert system. That reframe unlocked everything else.

I also learned that doing most of the design work solo on a group project is both a strength and a risk. The output was consistent because I owned it — but there were probably ideas that didn't make it in.

What's Next

A real working Focus Block is the obvious next step — the 3D model exists, now it needs actual hardware. Beyond that: testing the reflection prompt flow with real users to see if it actually reduces drop-off, and exploring how the cube syncs streaks in real time without feeling intrusive.

The concept is solid. It just needs to be built.
